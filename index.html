FSRDC Research Output Dashboard

This dashboard summarizes key insights from our CIT 5900 Project 3 analysis of Federal Statistical Research Data Center (FSRDC) outputs. Interactive charts let you explore the data — captions highlight the main take-aways.

Methodology & Analysis

Data Enrichment & Curation

• Loaded 8 project metadata Excel sheets (AllMetadata, Researchers, Datasets, Abstracts, ResearchOutput2024) via pandas.
• Merged on title and PI; standardized 17 key fields; removed duplicates and imputed missing values.
• Generated enrichment keywords (RDC names, “FSRDC”, “Census Bureau”) to flag true FSRDC outputs.
• Applied exact & fuzzy matching (threshold of 90) on author names and titles to filter out non-FSRDC records.
• Queried the OpenAlex API (no additional APIs) by title/DOI to pull DOI, citation counts, publication dates, abstracts & topical concepts.
• Web-scraped RePEc Census Working Papers for any missing records, using the same cleaning & acknowledgment-keyword criteria.

Exploratory Data Analysis

  • Top 10 RDCs by output volume: generated a bar chart showing the leading centers.
  • Publication trends over time: plotted Year (vs) # of publications to reveal growth, 2021 dip, and 2023 rebound.
  • Top 10 prolific authors: ranked by number of outputs and by total citations (h-index & raw counts).
  • Citation insights: used scatter/density plots to surface high-impact outliers and typical citation trajectories.
  • Merged our curated “New” dataset with the 2024 ResearchOutput.xlsx to compare coverage and extend the official list.

Classification & Topic Modeling

• Preprocessed abstracts (lowercase, remove stopwords/punctuation); replaced missing abstracts with titles.
• Used a fine-tuned BERT classifier to assign each paper to one of five topic categories (e.g. Demographic Data, Economic Growth).
• Analyzed topic prevalence over time: plotted percent of outputs per topic by year.

Statistical Modeling & Regression Analysis

  • Linear Regression: Modeled citation count as a function of days-since-publication, topic dummies, and their interactions. Time-since-publication significantly interacts with Efficiency, Productivity & Economic Growth topics (p < 0.01), driving higher citation counts.
  • The model’s R²≈0.07 indicates modest explanatory power, highlighting additional factors—journal prestige, author networks, etc.—that lie outside our feature set.

Network Analysis

• Converted each record into a ResearchOutput object; built an undirected graph linking nodes sharing the same RDC & topic.
• Graph has 918 nodes & 13,635 edges; identified 51 connected components, with the largest (N=686) centered on Boston & Demographic Data outputs.

Key Insights

  • Journal articles drive both the highest volume and extreme citation outliers (10 k+ cites).
  • High output volume does not guarantee high impact—some working papers and datasets show low citation curves.
  • Efficiency & Productivity topics accrue citations faster over time compared to Demographic or Infrastructure topics.
  • Boston, Penn State, and Yale not only publish the most but also host the most highly cited research.

Supplementary Figures from Modeling

Number of Outputs per Year by BERTopic

Urban Studies (cyan) has dominated FSRDC research since about 2012, surging to over 115 outputs in 2018 and remaining the top topic through 2024. The Firms topic (brown) also saw rapid growth, peaking around 2016–17, while General Economics (pink) and Healthcare (gray) climbed steadily after 2010. By contrast, niche areas like Crime (orange), Education (red) and Environment (purple) stay at low single‐digit counts. This shift suggests a growing emphasis on urban and firm‐level analysis in recent years.

Evolution of Topics Across Years

Demographic Data & Household Structures (blue) dominated FSRDC outputs in the early 1990s and has since stabilized around 50–70 % of publications per year. Growth, Firm and Productivity (green) surged to 100 % briefly in the mid-’90s, dipped around 2000, and then climbed back to roughly 20–30 % after 2005. Efficiency, Productivity and Economic Growth (red) peaked at 50 % in the early ’90s but fell to near zero by 2010, indicating a waning focus on pure economic‐growth topics. Human Capital, Labor Force and Productivity (purple) appears only after 2000 and remains a niche < 15 % share.
